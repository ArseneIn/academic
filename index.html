1. Personal Reflections

A summary of my learning journey, key insights, and challenges encountered during the module.

Grasping Core Concepts

Over this module I deepened my grasp of core research‐methodology concepts—particularly mixed‐methods design, sampling strategies, and ethical protocols. Aligning qualitative interviews with quantitative surveys helped me frame a cohesive case‐study approach for boutique owners in Murama Cell, ensuring I could explore both measurable patterns and lived experiences.

My Iterative Learning Process

My learning process was iterative. I struggled to balance breadth (“What is DFL?”) with depth (“How does it translate into record keeping?”). I drafted multiple versions of my problem statement and sought peer feedback—an exercise that clarified my main objective: assessing how DFL influences boutique management and informing a tailored digital tool.

Overcoming Challenges

The biggest challenge was crafting focused research objectives. My first draft contained five; after reflection I consolidated them into three precise, actionable items. This process taught me the value of paring back complexity to ensure feasibility.

2. Methodological Tools

A breakdown of the research techniques applied in my proposal.

I chose a single-case study of boutique owners in Murama Cell to capture both numerical trends and rich personal insights, integrating quantitative surveys with qualitative interviews. This design allowed me to measure, for example, the percentage of owners using mobile money for record-keeping, while also exploring in depth how they interpret and apply digital tools in daily operations. By focusing on one well-defined community, I could iteratively compare survey results against interview narratives, ensuring that my findings reflected both breadth and depth of DFL’s impact.

I first applied purposive sampling to recruit 59 boutique owners who had been operating for at least six months and managed their shops independently, ensuring relevance to my objectives. Recognizing that some informal traders lacked formal listings, I then employed snowball sampling, asking each participant to refer peers within Murama. This two-stage approach widened my reach to include owners outside official channels, enriching the diversity of digital-literacy experiences captured.

To quantify DFL and management practices, I created a mostly close-ended questionnaire in English and Kinyarwanda, covering:

  • Demographics (age, education)
  • Use and understanding of mobile-money and banking apps
  • Record-keeping habits (manual vs. digital)
  • Challenges in financial and inventory management

    • Attitudes toward adopting a bespoke digital tool I will administer this instrument in person—providing reading/writing assistance where needed—to 59 owners, allowing me to compute correlations between DFL levels and record-keeping accuracy

    Complementing the survey, I will conduct 10–15 minute interviews with a purposive subset of owners to explore nuances behind their questionnaire responses. I will ask open questions about their motivations, fears, and feature preferences for a digital management app. With participants’ permission, I will audio-record and later transcribe each session, coding themes such as “trust in technology” and “language barriers,” which illuminated why some owners resisted even simple digital workflows

    I will ensure ethical rigor by obtaining informed consent in writing, explaining my study’s purpose, voluntary nature, and data-handling procedures. I will also anonymize all responses, storing identifiers separately under password protection, and secured formal approval from Kepler College’s Research Department before data collection . Throughout, I remained attentive to participants’ well-being—allowing anyone to withdraw at any time without consequence.

    3. Proposal & Literature Reflection

    An analysis of my research proposal components and their relation to existing literature.

    Literature Review Insights

    My review highlighted a key gap: while DFL is recognized as critical (OECD, 2021), most studies focus on urban businesses, overlooking peri-urban owners in Rwanda. The literature provided a theoretical base but failed to account for the nuanced realities on the ground, reinforcing my decision to conduct field observations.

    Expert Review Questions

    1. Are my problem statement and research objectives clearly linked and logically aligned?
    2. Is the mixed-method design appropriate and practicable for this context?
    3. Does the application of Rogers’s Diffusion of Innovations theory adequately frame adoption barriers?
    4. Will my final recommendations be actionable and sustainable for the boutique owners?

    Proposal Component Reflections

    Introduction & Context

    I began with compelling statistics (e.g., “70% of small businesses lack formal record-keeping”) to create an impactful and concise background.

    Problem Statement

    Iterating between national reports and local observations helped me frame a precise problem: owners have smartphones but lack skills for business use.

    Significance

    I highlighted both academic contributions (filling the “urban bias” gap) and practical ones (a co-created tool) to show tangible impact.

    Limitations

    Acknowledging location and sample-size constraints strengthened credibility. I planned triangulation of data to ensure robust findings.
